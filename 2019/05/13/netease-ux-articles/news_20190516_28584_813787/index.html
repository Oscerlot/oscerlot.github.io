On May 13th, the eighth lecture of the joint course “Service Innovation Design” by NetEase Leihuo User Experience Center and Zhejiang University (ZJU) gave a detailed lecture on iteration risks and interaction prototypes.

This course is mainly divided into three parts: UI iteration risks, introduction and case sharing of interaction prototypes, and tool introduction. It is taught by the interaction design manager of Leihuo UX.

The following are part of the course content:

UI Iteration Risks

  1. Major experience problems are discovered only after launch.
  2. An important function has always had experience problems, but it has not been solved.
  3. Insufficient innovation in the interaction scheme of core functions.

How to Avoid Iteration Risks

Iteration is the norm in the process of game development.

Avoid it by making prototypes for testing.

Who is the Prototype for?

Decision makers - Decision-making

Experiencers - Testing

Designers themselves - Self-testing

Good interface, “looks good” or “smooth” is not the ultimate goal, the ultimate goal is to make players feel that their experience is under control, and the prototype is a means to advance the experience.

Service Design Tools Introduction

01. Customer Journey Map

 

At the heart of most customer experience research, journey maps track user-facing interactions with services and represent them as structured visualizations. Typical journey maps are multi-channel and time-based. Creating it requires researchers to identify the touchpoints where users interact with the service, connect these touchpoints as part of the overall experience, and depict the various stages of the experience for further analysis.

02. Service Blueprint

 

As a key tool for service design, blueprints depict each component of the service, including users and service providers, their touchpoints, and behind-the-scenes processes. Unlike customer journey maps, these blueprints capture the work of the provider and the key processes of service delivery. Because they run through the user experience, service blueprints can reveal where silos exist in the organization. According to Dana Chisnell, co-founder of the Center for Civic Design, the gaps between these silos often correspond to low points in customer service. Service blueprints are designed to be co-generated and can be a tool to bring together different teams responsible for the discontinuous parts of the service. The process of creating a blueprint itself can foster collaboration and can also reveal areas of overlap and teamwork. Pollaine further suggests creating Journey Summaries, tracking a particular user’s journey through the main stages of the service in order to select touchpoints to focus on.

03. Stakeholder Map

 

Stakeholder maps identify important internal and external stakeholders and key users, and often describe the relationships between stakeholders and their relative importance. In addition to service design, stakeholder mapping is also used in strategy analysis, project management, and other fields. It helps the team assess how the interests of each stakeholder affect the project and how to address these issues.

04. Expectation Map

Expectation maps build on customer journey maps as a way to visualize customer emotions and desires during the journey. They are usually created during the discovery phase and are designed to represent the dynamically changing expectations of the service, expressing the best experience users can imagine. The map captures expectations in four aspects: seeing, hearing, saying, and doing, as well as thinking and feeling. The map can take a deep look at specific channels or aspects of the service to define where more details are needed. It can be used as a diagnostic tool to pinpoint areas of service that need attention.

05. Customer Lifecycle Map

 

By providing a comprehensive understanding of the customer’s relationship with the service provider, customer lifecycle maps are often used to highlight points where people may abandon the service. It takes into account the customer’s motivation and momentum overtime and can be described as a series of service journeys.

06. Design Scenario

This tool can combine several other techniques to integrate storyboards or characters into a hypothetical story. Driven by user research conducted by the team, the scenario can be particularly helpful in examining potential problems that a new service concept may encounter, or in constructing negative scenarios, exploring what might get worse to highlight what works well in practice. Design scenarios can also help teams build around a clear vision.

07. Organizational Impact Analysis

This additional tool is provided to establish a direct link between the customer experience and the delivery mechanism. It identifies the impact of customer stages or channels on different departments. This analysis is designed to help teams plan collaboration with internal stakeholders and pave the way for change.

08. Business Model Canvas

 

The canvas replaces the traditional business plan to identify the elements of a sustainable service product. Although the canvas is usually applied to private sector services, public sector organizations can use this tool to help departments see themselves as service-centric businesses, including how their key activities support a value proposition that serves specific customer relationships. The business model canvas contains nine sectors that can be filled in collaboratively to help service providers simulate various aspects of their service offerings.
