Introduction

Gestalt principles are a fundamental theory in design, widely used in many aspects of visual design and interaction design.

Let’s take a look at Gestalt principles to make design easier.

What are Gestalt Principles?

Gestalt is the transliteration of the German word “Gestalt”, which means form, shape, or structure.

Gestalt principles are actually a psychological concept, also known as Gestalt psychology.

Gestalt psychology mainly studies the visual and sensory fields, advocating the integrity of perception.

The human vision has a tendency to generalize and simplify graphics.

In other words, when we look at things, the eyes and brain work together.

It is not to distinguish the individual components of an image at the beginning, but to combine the various parts into a more easily understood unity.

Gestalt principles include multiple principles. Here we mainly share four of them: similarity, proximity, closure, and continuity.

 

① Principle of Similarity

The meaning of the principle of similarity is very simple, that is, elements that are similar in a certain aspect tend to form a whole.

For example, in the figure below, people’s first impression of this picture is 2 kinds of circles, not 15 circles.

This is because our eyes and brains automatically group the solid circles into one group and the hollow circles into another group.

 

People usually think of it as 2 kinds of circles instead of 15 circles

When elements are similar in shape, size, or color, the principle of similarity can work.

For example, in the figure below, we can easily distinguish between similar and different, and group similar ones together.

 

Group similar graphics together

② Principle of Proximity

The principle of proximity means that elements that are close in distance or position tend to form a whole.

As shown in the figure below, we will naturally think that these are 3 groups of circles, not 6 independent circles.

 

We will naturally think that these are 3 groups of circles, not 6 independent circles

Proximity is actually the primary principle of Gestalt, and its influence is so great that it can offset other principles.

As shown in the figure below, even if the shapes are not similar or the colors are not similar, as long as the distance between the elements is close enough, we will be more inclined to regard them as a group.

 

When the distance between elements is close enough, they tend to be regarded as a whole even if they are not similar

Generally speaking, proximity > color similarity > shape similarity. The arrangement of the three groups of patterns in the figure below can well reflect this point.

Therefore, in interface design, we often use the principle of proximity to distinguish large functional modules, and then use color similarity to distinguish states (such as selected states, etc.).

 

Proximity > Color Similarity > Shape Similarity

Application of Similarity Principle and Proximity Principle

In practical applications, similarity and proximity are usually used together.

The simplest principles are often the most powerful and useful. Similarity and proximity are widely used in various UI designs.

① Control Layout

The navigation bar, which is used by almost all apps, contains the principles of similarity and proximity.

Take the navigation bar of the “NetEase OA” app as an example.

The form and size of each group of tabs are similar, and users can easily identify that they belong to the same functional module.

At the same time, the color of the selected tab is different, highlighting its different state (this is also the reason why we often use color to represent special states such as selected states).

And when we analyze these, we actually default that these are five groups of elements.

Although the similarity between the icon and the text is very low, because they are close enough, we will default that they are a whole.

 

The control layout of the navigation bar cleverly uses Gestalt principles

② Functional Partitioning

In game interface design, proximity and similarity are also very important.

Because a game interface often contains multiple functional modules, proximity can help us perform functional partitioning.

For example, the deployment interface of Conqueror’s Blade.

We will separate different functional modules, so that the functional division of the entire interface is very obvious.

The most important deploy button is separated from other functional blocks, and the prominent size and color are used to make it easier for players to notice the button.

In the same functional module, such as the densely arranged information area on the left, the distance between rows is also separated to clarify that each row is the information of the same player.

At the same time, the similar icon forms and arrangement positions make it easier for players to identify similar information and compare information.

 

Gestalt principles help with functional partitioning within the game interface

③ Information Clustering

The principles of proximity and similarity can also help us cluster information and show the differences and connections between large amounts of information.

For example, the Soul interface of Conqueror’s Blade contains a lot of grid and icon information, which uses the distance between different grids for clustering.

At the same time, size similarity is used to enhance the distinction. The larger ones in the middle are the installation slots, and the two sides are the Soul libraries.

The principle of symmetry is also implied here. The symmetrical form will make people feel more comfortable and holistic.

In addition, the background pattern of the installed Soul and the background pattern of the slot are the same, which builds a connection between them through shape similarity.

 

Using Gestalt principles can show the differences and connections between large amounts of information

④ Use Containers

Sometimes there is too much information in the same interface, and there is no space to separate them.

In this case, in addition to using distance for clustering, we can also use “containers” to frame the same group of information in one area.

The container can be a background or a line.

For example, the tournament interface of UNO! needs to accommodate a large amount of information. In order to be able to clearly classify and display this information, we use different backgrounds.

In addition, the score panel here uses the principle of proximity to separate “Classic Single Player” and “Fun Single Player”.

Color similarity is used to distinguish between positive and negative score levels.

 

Gestalt principles can clearly classify and display large amounts of information
