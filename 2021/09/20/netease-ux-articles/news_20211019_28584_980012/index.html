Introduction

In user questionnaire analysis, sometimes we face a situation:

Fashion A: 8 out of 13 players voted “like,” 5 voted “dislike.”
Fashion B: 16 out of 28 players “like,” 12 “dislike.”
Fashion C: 135 out of 270 players “like,” 135 “dislike.”

At this point, how should we choose which fashion is more popular with players?

Method 1: Look at the net “like” count, i.e., “like” count - “dislike” count. In this case, Fashion B should be the most popular.

Method 2: Look at the “like” rate, i.e., “like” count / (“like” count + “dislike” count). In this case, Fashion A should be the most popular.

Is there perhaps a more convincing method?

Besides wondering why so many people filled out the questionnaire for Fashion C, while only a few people filled it out for Fashions A and B, this problem actually contains knowledge about sample size and statistics. One solution to this problem is the Wilson score, which cleverly applies the knowledge of statistical interval estimation to content evaluation, combining sample size and player evaluation to obtain a fairer and more effective evaluation score.

What is the Wilson Score?

Strictly speaking, the Wilson score can be called the lower bound of the confidence interval for the binomial distribution interval estimation. It makes the following assumptions about players’ voting behavior:

  • Each player’s vote is an independent event.
  • Players’ voting results are only “like” and “dislike”.

Under such assumptions, voting “like” or “dislike” is transformed into a binomial distribution in probability. By collecting player questionnaires, we can estimate the player’s “like” rate p for a certain outfit. That is, in n independent votes, there are k votes for “like”. By using the actual values of n and k, we can evaluate the relationship between k/n and p.

Intuitively, when n is larger, k/n will be closer to p, and when n is smaller, the result of k/n may lack persuasiveness.

Confidence intervals and interval estimation are a type of parameter estimation. For example, regarding the player’s preference rate p for a certain fashion item, unless we ask every player if they really like a certain fashion item, we can never get all players’ opinions on a certain fashion item. Therefore, we need to analyze the results of the questionnaires already obtained to infer the relationship between the player’s questionnaire preference rate k/n and the player’s true preference rate p in the existing results. Since it is an inference or estimation, there are two situations: overestimation and underestimation. The entire interval connecting underestimation and overestimation is interval estimation. In contrast, point estimation only estimates one result.

The lower bound, as the name suggests, is an underestimation of the parameter to be estimated. Only with strong evidence can we face up to the results of, for example, 95% or 99% preference rate generated from the questionnaire. Otherwise, under a small statistical sample size, the 95% preference rate will be greatly reduced.
