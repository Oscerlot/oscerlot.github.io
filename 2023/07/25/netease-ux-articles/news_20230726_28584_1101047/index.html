2. “Dopamine Dressing” in Ancient Chinese Art and Ancient Style Games

In fact, as early as in ancient China, there were “dopamine” styles in the Terracotta Army, Tang Sancai, and Tang tomb murals. For example, the Terracotta Army, in fact, their original colors when they were just unearthed were very rich. There were 8 colors in total, including red, green, black, white, blue, purple, yellow, and pink, with nearly 20 shades on different parts of the clothing. These colored pigments came from various minerals or plants in nature, and the color matching was also very bold and avant-garde. There are also a few synthetic materials in the pigments, such as “Chinese purple”, “lead white” and “lead red”, which are considered to be artificially made.

It is understood that scientists first discovered barium copper silicate on Han Dynasty pottery in 1992 and called it “Han purple”. Later, it was found that it was widely used on the Qin Terracotta Army, so it is now generally called “Chinese purple”. The thousands of faces of the Terracotta Army are also vivid and lively due to the “dopamine” color dressing under the careful dressing of ancient craftsmen. Chinese purple is also used by some game art designers to extract design inspiration.

 

The “dopamine dressing” style of the Han Dynasty was based on classical elegance. Hanfu and Han etiquette showed the elegance in the hearts of Chinese people. The main colors of clothing were white, khaki, and brown, embellished with red and pink.

The Tang Dynasty had a broad vision, broad mind and open attitude in terms of political and economic systems, culture and art, and social atmosphere. It absorbed foreign cultures, innovated and developed, and led China into a new era. The Tang Sancai human figurines preserved in many museums in Shaanxi also show the popular “dopamine” dressing at that time.
